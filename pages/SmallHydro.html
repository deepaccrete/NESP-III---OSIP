Sector Overview

Powering Nigeria's Renewable Future

Nigeria has one of Africa’s strongest solar energy potentials due to its high solar radiation levels and growing demand for reliable electricity. Nigeria's solar market is the country's largest clean energy opportunity, driven by abundant sunlight and strong demand for reliable off-grid and embedded power solutions. The rising energy costs are accelerating the adoption of solar technologies across residential, commercial, industrial, and public-sector applications.

Solar projects in Nigeria span utility-scale generation, mini-grids, solar home systems, and commercial and industrial installations. The sector continues to attract local and international investors as the country expands access to clean and reliable energy.

Business Process & Incentives

Ease of Doing Business

Nigeria offers one of Africa's most accessible solar investment environments, supported by strong market demand, improving regulatory clarity, and favourable investment policies. Company incorporation through the Corporate Affairs Commission (CAC) is typically completed within 1–7 days, while 100% foreign ownership is permitted across the solar value chain. Investors may register with the Nigerian Investment Promotion Commission (NIPC) to access fiscal incentives, investment facilitation services, and other government-backed investment support programmes.

The Electricity Act 2023 has expanded opportunities for private-sector participation in electricity generation, distribution, mini-grids, embedded generation, and distributed energy systems. Solar projects can be developed under multiple business models, including utility-scale projects, commercial and industrial (C&I) installations, mini-grids, solar home systems, and solar-plus-storage solutions.

Nigeria's investment framework also provides attractive fiscal incentives for renewable energy projects. Eligible investors may access a 3–5-year Pioneer Status tax holiday, which may be extended subject to performance conditions. Renewable energy equipment may also benefit from VAT incentives and import-duty concessions under applicable renewable energy policies and ECOWAS Common External Tariff (CET) provisions, helping to reduce project development costs.

Nigeria further benefits from established regulatory frameworks for grid-connected and off-grid projects, growing local technical capacity, increasing availability of project financing, and active support from development partners such as REA, GIZ, AfDB, the World Bank, IFC, and other climate-finance institutions.

Regulatory Landscape

Regulations

Applicable Laws & Policies

Solar energy projects in Nigeria are primarily governed by the Electricity Act 2023, National Integrated Electricity Policy (NIEP) 2025, National Renewable Energy and Energy Efficiency Policy (NREEEP) 2015, Renewable Energy Master Plan (REMP), Energy Transition Plan (ETP) 2022, and Nigeria's updated Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs). These policies support private-sector participation, renewable energy deployment, and long-term decarbonization objectives.

Technical Standards

Solar equipment imported into Nigeria must comply with applicable SON standards and certification requirements, including SONCAP where applicable. Electrical installations are subject to NEMSA inspection and certification requirements. Grid-connected projects must comply with the NERC Grid Code and TCN interconnection standards.

Capital & Investment

Finance

Public Finance

Nigeria's solar sector benefits from growing public and development-finance support. The Rural Electrification Agency (REA), through the Rural Electrification Fund (REF), provides grants, subsidies, and performance-based incentives for mini-grid and solar access projects. The US$750 million DARES Programme supports isolated and interconnected mini-grids, Solar for Business initiatives, and productive-use energy projects.

The Bank of Industry (BOI) provides concessional financing for renewable energy manufacturing, deployment, and productive-use applications, while the NSIA-anchored US$500 million Distributed Renewable Energy (DRE) Nigeria Fund supports mini-grids, solar home systems, commercial and industrial (C&I) solar, and battery storage projects.

Commercial Finance

Commercial financing for solar is expanding rapidly. Nigerian financial institutions including Access Bank, FCMB, Sterling Bank, Fidelity Bank, Ecobank, and Lotus Bank now offer renewable energy financing products for project developers, businesses, and end-users.

Access Bank FCMB Sterling Bank Fidelity Bank Ecobank Lotus Bank

The Lotus Bank–REA ₦100 billion revolving credit facility provides counterpart financing for DARES-qualified developers, while commercial lenders increasingly support solar deployment across manufacturing, agriculture, healthcare, telecommunications, and commercial real estate sectors.

International financiers including IFC, AfDB, Africa Finance Corporation (AFC), Africa50, and infrastructure-focused investors are also increasing debt and equity participation across the Nigerian solar market.

Guarantees & Risk Instruments

InfraCredit guarantees, NSIA-backed de-risking mechanisms, political risk insurance, and blended-finance structures are increasingly being used to unlock local-currency financing and reduce project risk.

Additional support is available through institutions such as MIGA, IFC, AfDB, and the World Bank Group, particularly for large-scale infrastructure, mini-grid, and distributed renewable energy investments.

Blended Finance

Blended finance remains the dominant funding structure for many solar projects. Programmes such as DARES, REF, and the DRE Nigeria Fund combine grants, concessional finance, private capital, and risk-sharing instruments to improve project bankability and crowd in private investment.

Emerging facilities such as the Renewable Energy Blended Finance Facility (REBF) are also expected to expand access to long-term capital for mini-grids, productive-use energy, agricultural electrification, and storage projects.

Infrastructure Concession Arrangements

Concession arrangements are increasingly being used to deliver solar energy projects in Nigeria through models that allocate project development, financing, and operational responsibility to private investors. Key structures adopted include:

Build–Own–Operate (BOO) mini-grid concessions Independent Power Producer (IPP) PPPs supported by long-term PPAs Franchise-style distributed solar concessions (e.g., Solar Power Naija)

These frameworks allow investors to retain ownership, control cash flows, and operate assets over long durations, while leveraging government-backed risk mitigation mechanisms. Nigeria has an active portfolio of concession-backed solar projects across three main segments:

a

Decentralized Mini-Grid Concessions

Over 125 solar mini-grids already deployed under the Nigeria Electrification Project, alongside ongoing expansion under programmes such as DARES and AMP, involving multiple private developers across states. These operate under exclusive service-area concessions, generating revenue from end-user tariffs and performance-based subsidies.

b

Utility-Scale Solar PPPs (IPP Model)

Projects structured around long-term Power Purchase Agreements (PPAs), government or quasi-sovereign offtakers, and project finance structures compatible with international lenders.

c

Distributed Solar and Franchise Models

Programmes such as Solar Power Naija aim to deploy millions of solar connections nationwide via private operators, offering rapid market penetration, retail-scale recurring revenue streams, and lower reliance on grid infrastructure.

Mini-Grid Development

One of the largest immediate opportunities is the Distributed Access through Renewable Energy Scale-up (DARES) programme, supported by a US$750 million World Bank facility and implemented by the Rural Electrification Agency (REA). The programme targets the deployment of approximately 1,225 mini-grids, 465 MW of new generation capacity, and electricity access for 17.5 million beneficiaries.

Private developers can participate through multiple windows, including isolated mini-grids, interconnected mini-grids, minimum subsidy tenders, and Solar for Business programmes. Performance-based subsidies are available upon successful project delivery and verified customer connections.

Commercial & Industrial Solar

Commercial and industrial (C&I) solar remains one of the fastest-growing segments of the market. Rising diesel costs, energy-security concerns, and sustainability commitments are driving demand for solar and solar-plus-storage solutions across manufacturing, agriculture, telecommunications, healthcare, education, and commercial real estate sectors.

Manufacturing Agriculture Telecommunications Healthcare Education Commercial Real Estate

Manufacturing & Local Assembly

Solar manufacturing, component assembly, and clean energy supply chains are emerging as strategic growth areas. Ongoing policy discussions around domestic industrialization, regional value chains, and local content development are creating opportunities for investors interested in module assembly, battery systems, mounting structures, inverters, and related clean-energy technologies.

Geographic Hotspots

High-opportunity markets continue to emerge across Lagos, Abuja, Kano, Kaduna, and key agricultural corridors in North-Central Nigeria, where strong electricity demand, productive-use activities, and electrification need support project viability.
